Project 02 — Computational Modelling

Thermo-Electric FE
Simulation Case Study

Finite element modelling of heat-enabled composite materials for next-generation flexible electronics. Developed at HP-NTU Corporate Lab, this work contributed to a filed US patent and reduced physical prototype iterations by 30%.

ABAQUS FEM Thermal Modelling Patent Filed MATLAB

Problem Statement

Next-generation flexible electronics require materials that can conduct heat directionally while maintaining mechanical compliance. Designing these thermo-electric composites through physical prototyping alone is slow and cost-prohibitive — each iteration requires fabrication, thermal imaging, and electrical characterisation.

The objective was to build a validated finite element model that could predict composite performance before physical build, reducing the design cycle and enabling principled material selection.

Modelling Approach

Model Architecture: Coupled thermo-electric FE models were built in ABAQUS using representative volume element (RVE) methodology. The models captured heat flux, electrical conductivity, and interfacial thermal resistance between composite constituents.

Boundary Conditions: Applied current-voltage loads matched experimental set-up configurations from bench-scale IV measurements. Thermal boundary conditions replicated environmental chamber test profiles (−20°C to 80°C operating range).

Validation Strategy: Predicted thermal distributions were validated against IR thermal imaging data captured on fabricated prototypes. Agreement within ±8% was achieved across 30+ composite assemblies.

Key Findings

The model successfully predicted thermal hotspot locations that corresponded to observed delamination failure modes. This identified interfacial bond strength as the critical design parameter — shifting formulation effort to adhesion chemistry rather than bulk conductivity.

Relevance to Forensic Fire Science

Thermal and thermo-electric FE modelling shares core methodology with computational fire investigation: both require accurate representation of heat transfer, material response under thermal loading, and boundary condition validation against physical measurements. The FMEA-driven approach used here — identifying and isolating failure modes through combined simulation and experiment — directly maps to fire origin and cause analysis, where understanding material ignition thresholds, heat flux pathways, and electrical fault signatures is central to the investigation process.
